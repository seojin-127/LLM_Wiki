## One-line Summary
A widely cited *Trends in Neurosciences* review (Novarino lab, IST Austria) that crystallised the **convergence framing** of NDDs — despite enormous genetic heterogeneity, NDD risk genes converge onto three core molecular pathways (PI3K-mTOR, transcriptional/epigenetic regulation, synaptic signaling) that interlock as a "multipathway loop", with gene vulnerability + mutational load + the two-hit model setting individual phenotypic complexity.

## 2. Key Contributions
- **Argues for the convergence framing** — the empirical observation that NDD-associated genes, despite their hundreds of identities and modes of action, cluster onto **three pathway families**:
  1. **Protein synthesis** — the PI3K-mTOR axis ("mTORopathies"): TSC1, TSC2, PTEN, DEPDC5, NPRL2, NPRL3
  2. **Transcriptional / epigenetic regulation** — chromatin remodelers and TFs: MECP2, SETD5, CHD2/4/8, ARID1B, ASH1L, KMT2A
  3. **Synaptic signaling** — cell-adhesion molecules + postsynaptic scaffolds: NRXN1, NLGN3/4, SHANK1/2/3
- **Frames the multipathway loop** — these three pathways are not parallel silos; they feedback through proteins that bridge them (e.g., FMRP / CYFIP1 connecting mTOR translation to synaptic protein production; Shank3 haploinsufficiency causing histone acetylation changes; SHANK2 transcriptomes enriched for FMRP targets). Mutations in any one pathway propagate through the loop.
- **Introduces gene vulnerability + mutational load + two-hit** as the framework for individual phenotypic variability:
  - *Gene vulnerability* = how poorly a gene tolerates LoF (haploinsufficient = highly vulnerable). Vulnerable genes have fewer LoF variants in population data (purifying selection).
  - *Mutational load* = total disruptive-variant burden across the genome
  - *Two-hit model* = inherited germline variant primes vulnerability; somatic second hit triggers the full phenotype (e.g., germline DEPDC5 LoF → epilepsy; +somatic biallelic DEPDC5 → focal cortical dysplasia)
- **Provides a comprehensive haploinsufficient mouse + human iPSC/organoid model table** (Table 1) for TSC1/TSC2/DEPDC5 — the most thorough comparative summary of mTORopathy models at the time.

## 3. Conceptual Architecture (the framework, in lieu of methods)
This is a review; the architecture is the three-pathway organising thesis applied to ~50 NDD-implicated genes.

### Genetic-architecture priors (Section: Genetics of NDDs)
Six modes of NDD genetic causation (Figure 1):
- **A** Mutational load × gene vulnerability (the master axis)
- **B** *De novo* heterozygous (most common pattern in syndromic NDDs)
- **C** Autosomal recessive (consanguineous / less frequent)
- **D** Dual molecular diagnoses (multiple causal events at distinct loci, *de novo* or inherited)
- **E** Two-hit (inherited primer + somatic second hit)
- **F** Polygenic / cumulative common-variant burden

### Pathway 1 — Protein synthesis (PI3K-mTOR / mTORopathies)
- mTOR = serine/threonine kinase, ubiquitous, two complexes (mTORC1, mTORC2)
- Three input arms: growth factor (PI3K-AKT-TSC), energy-sensing (AMPK-TSC), amino-acid-sensing (Rag GTPases / GATOR1)
- Negative regulators that, when mutated, hyperactivate mTORC1: TSC1, TSC2, PTEN, DEPDC5, NPRL2, NPRL3
- mTOR targets in development: progenitor proliferation, differentiation, neurite outgrowth, mRNA translation
- Adult-brain mTOR roles: learning, memory, circuit refinement, synaptic plasticity
- Phenotype distinction by which arm is hit: TSC1/TSC2/PTEN/AKT mutations → ASD + ID + epilepsy; GATOR1 (DEPDC5/NPRL) mutations → focal epilepsy
- TSC clinical: tubers in multiple organs; 50% ASD, 50–60% ID, 80–90% early-onset seizures
- Mouse models: Tsc1+/−, Tsc2+/− show memory deficits + ASD-like phenotypes but NOT tumors/seizures (haploinsufficient mice underrecapitulate human tubers — points to need for second hit)
- Human iPSC + organoid models reveal: TSC2+/− → CLIP cells (caudal late interneuron progenitors, human-specific), founder population for tubers — second-hit mutations occur during progression, not initiation

### Pathway 2 — Transcriptional / epigenetic regulation
- NDD-implicated chromatin remodelers and TFs: MECP2, SETD5, CHD8, CHD1/2/4, ASH1L, ARID1B, KMT2A
- Pleiotropy is the defining feature — each chromatin regulator has many targets; differential expression studies reveal disturbed neuronal-development, chromatin, cell-cycle, RNA modules
- Disturbed gene modules are themselves enriched in NDD risk genes (chromatin mutation → indirect dysregulation of synaptic/mTOR-pathway genes → multipathway loop)
- CHD family: CHD1/CHD4 → ID; CHD2 → epileptic encephalopathy; CHD8 → ASD
- ARID1B (BAF complex): one of the most frequent NDD causes; controls interneuron migration + differentiation; haploinsufficient mice show specific PV-interneuron reduction
- SETD5: critical for early-development gene expression; haploinsufficient mice show synaptic protein dysregulation + cell-fate determination defects
- Mouse / human discordance: e.g., Chd8+/− mice have very mild phenotypes despite strong human ASD association; Chd4 cKO produces microcephaly in mice but human patients have macrocephaly + ID

### Pathway 3 — Synaptic signaling
- Two functional groups: cell-adhesion molecules (CAMs — NRXN, NLGN) for trans-cellular bidirectional organisation; scaffolding/signaling proteins (SHANK family) at PSD
- NRXN1 LoF in human neurons → synaptic impairment + dysregulated NT release
- NLGN4 missense → increased excitatory synapses
- SHANK2 LoF → hyperconnectivity of excitatory neurons
- SHANK3 → multipathway-loop poster child: SHANK3 transcriptome enriched in FMRP targets + chromatin/transcriptional regulators; SHANK3 patient also carries CYFIP1 deletion (CYFIP1 = FMRP partner that modulates mTOR-dependent translation + E/I balance); Shank3+/− mice show histone-acetylation abnormalities rescuable by HDAC inhibitor
- Suggests synaptic-pathway dysregulation propagates back to mTOR (via FMRP/CYFIP1) and chromatin (via histone acetylation) — closing the loop

## 5. Limitations & Outstanding Questions Acknowledged
The review's "Outstanding Questions" box explicitly raises:
- Can endogenous compensatory mechanisms be exploited as treatments? What are the secondary consequences?
- How translatable is NDD molecular knowledge to clinical interventions?
- Are there *common* prenatal-stage pathway disturbances that could serve as early-prognosis signatures?
- What are the most effective methods for NGS data analysis at the scale required?
- Is gene therapy realistic for NDDs? How limiting is the multifactorial complexity?

Limitations the review's framing has (apparent in retrospect):
- Environmental factors are *out of scope* — explicitly: "discussion of environmental factors is beyond the scope of the current review". This is precisely what [[dvir-2026-complex-genotype-phenotype-relationships-in|Dvir et al. 2026]] adds.
- Convergence-only framing leaves *intra-gene* phenotypic variability unaddressed (the inverse problem — same gene, different phenotypes).
- Mouse model / human discordance acknowledged but framing for resolution is mostly "use iPSC organoids" without quantitative cross-system metrics ([[overviews/brain-organoid-fidelity]] addresses this gap).

## 7. Glossary (paper's own glossary, condensed)
- **BAF complex** — BRG1/BRM-associated factor; ATP-dependent chromatin remodeler family (ARID1B is a structural subunit)
- **CLIP cells** — human-specific caudal late interneuron progenitors; characterised in organoids by late midgestational origin and COUP-TFII / PROX1 / EGFR expression; founder population of TSC tumors
- **Comorbidity** — co-occurrence of multiple NDD phenotypes (e.g., ID + ASD + epilepsy) in a single individual
- **Epistasis** — physical / functional interaction between gene products driving phenotype
- **Gene vulnerability** — capacity of a gene to tolerate disruptive variants (low tolerance = vulnerable = haploinsufficient)
- **Genetic counseling** — clinical guidance on disease risk, recurrence, and inheritance for affected families
- **Molecular diagnosis** — identification of the causal mutational event (vs. clinical diagnosis = phenotype-based recognition)
- **mTORopathies** — NDDs caused by deregulation of mTOR signaling (TSC, hemimegalencephaly, focal cortical dysplasia, etc.)
- **Multifactorial / polygenic disorder** — phenotype emerging from combination of genetic and nongenetic / multiple genetic events
- **Multipathway loop** — neurons integrate synaptic, dendritic, and nuclear events through cellular feedback; mutations in one pathway perturb the others
- **Mutational load** — total number of disruptive variants across the genome
- **PI3K-mTOR pathway** — phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase / mammalian target of rapamycin signaling controlling cell survival, proliferation, migration, metabolism
- **Two-hit model** — inherited germline mutation + later somatic second hit jointly producing the disease phenotype
