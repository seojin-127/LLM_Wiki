## One-line Summary
Brain Cell Atlas: 26.3M cells from 70 human + 103 mouse studies across major developmental stages and brain regions; ML-based consensus annotation; discovers PCDH9-high microglia with hippocampus-PFC regulatory differences.

## 1. Document Info
- Journal/Conference: Nature Medicine
- Published: September 2024

## 2. Key Contributions
- Brain Cell Atlas: 26.3M cells from 70 human + 103 mouse studies across major developmental stages
- Machine learning-based consensus cell type annotation across diverse datasets/protocols
- Identifies putative neural progenitor cells in human brain
- Discovers PCDH9-high microglia subpopulation; distinct regulatory programs in hippocampus vs. PFC
- Cell-cell communication network mapping across brain regions

## 3. Methods & Architecture
- Integration of 173 studies (70 human + 103 mouse) via ML-based consensus annotation
- Covers multiple developmental stages and all major brain regions
- PCDH9-high microglia: gene regulatory network comparison across hippocampus and PFC

## 4. Key Results & Benchmarks
- 26.3M cells = largest integrative human+mouse brain atlas
- ML consensus annotation resolves cross-dataset label inconsistencies
- Neural progenitor cells identified in adult human brain
- PCDH9-high microglia: region-specific gene regulatory differences between hippocampus and PFC
- Cell-cell communication network characterized across regions

## 5. Limitations & Future Work
- Integration artifacts possible across 173 datasets with diverse protocols
- ML annotation quality depends on reference label consistency
- PCDH9-high microglia functional significance not experimentally validated

## 6. Related Work
- He et al. 2024 (HNOCA): organoid fidelity benchmark against primary brain
- Yao et al. 2023: Allen Brain Cell Atlas (mouse reference)
- Langlieb et al. 2023: whole adult mouse brain atlas

## 7. Glossary
- **PCDH9**: Protocadherin-9; cell adhesion molecule expressed in neural and immune cells
- **Consensus annotation**: ML approach to harmonize cell type labels across studies with different nomenclatures
