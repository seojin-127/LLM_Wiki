## One-line Summary
Landmark paper establishing cerebral organoids from hPSCs: self-organizing 3D brain tissue with discrete regional identities, oRG-like cells, and cortical neuron subtypes; models microcephaly via patient iPSCs and RNAi.

## 1. Document Info
- Journal/Conference: Nature
- Published: August 2013

## 2. Key Contributions
- First human pluripotent stem cell-derived 3D cerebral organoid system
- Develops discrete brain regions including cortex, retinal pigment, choroid plexus, meninges-like
- Contains outer radial glia (oRG)-like cells characteristic of human cortical expansion
- Patient iPSC and RNAi model of primary microcephaly (CDK5RAP2): premature differentiation
- Established the organoid paradigm for human brain disease modeling

## 3. Methods & Architecture
- hPSC embedded in Matrigel, then transferred to spinning bioreactor
- No directed patterning: self-organization produces multiple brain regions
- Histology, immunofluorescence, scRNA-seq of cell types

## 4. Key Results & Benchmarks
- Organoids develop VZ, SVZ, cortical plate-like regions with inside-out layering
- oRG-like cells present (characteristic of human OSVZ)
- Microcephaly patient organoids: premature neuronal differentiation explaining reduced brain size
- Organoids recapitulate human-specific progenitor zone organization

## 5. Limitations & Future Work
- Unguided: heterogeneous and variable between batches
- Lacks vascularization → necrotic core in large organoids
- No reproducible regional identity; later methods (Pasca 2015) addressed this with guided differentiation

## 6. Related Work
- Pasca et al. 2015: guided cortical spheroid protocol (more reproducible)
- Mansour et al. 2018: vascularized organoid (addresses necrosis)
- He et al. 2024: HNOCA organoid fidelity assessment

## 7. Glossary
- **oRG (outer radial glia)**: Human-specific progenitor in OSVZ driving cortical expansion
- **CDK5RAP2**: Centrosomal protein; mutations cause primary microcephaly (MCPH3)
- **Spinning bioreactor**: Culture vessel providing oxygen for large organoid growth
