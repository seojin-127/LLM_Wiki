## Summary
Human cortical organoids transplanted into newborn rat somatosensory cortex mature into complex cell types and integrate into sensory and motivation circuits. Receive thalamocortical + corticocortical inputs, show in vivo sensory responses, and drive reward-seeking behavior via optogenetic activation. Timothy syndrome circuit defects detected only in transplanted (not in vitro) setting.

## Key Contributions
- hCO → rat somatosensory cortex transplantation → mature neuron morphology, synaptics, membrane properties
- Activity-dependent transcriptional programs activated post-transplantation
- Thalamocortical + corticocortical inputs; sensory-evoked responses in human neurons
- Optogenetic activation → reward-seeking behavior (real-time place preference)
- Timothy syndrome: circuit defects uncovered (invisible in standard in vitro organoids)

## Methods & Architecture
- hCO transplanted into P1 athymic rats; MRI for growth tracking; snRNA-seq for maturation
- Anatomical tracing + in vivo electrophysiology + patch clamp + optogenetics

## Results
- In vivo maturation superior to in vitro for morphological and functional properties
- Human neurons receive and respond to sensory thalamocortical input
- Axons extend throughout rat brain; optogenetic activation drives behavior
- Timothy syndrome: aberrant activity patterns detectable only post-transplantation

## Limitations
- Immunocompromised host; neonatal transplantation window
